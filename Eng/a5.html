“F: Buenas noches, Manuel. Your skill in theater is remarkable. I admire your purity of heart and your courage.”
“D: Indeed, hija. Manuel is a true scholar at such a young age. One would hardly believe he has not yet reached eighteen.”
“M: Muchas gracias, Don Fernando and Señorita Felicita. Yet this nation suffers such sorrowful fate—it is in dire need of transformation, not only for today but for the generations yet to come.”
“F: Do you realize that if you continue to criticize the government, you may find yourself colliding with powerful politicians?”
“M: Since childhood I have known hardship. Governors who should have been helping us were instead our oppressors. The people were voiceless, and I became their voice.”
“F: You impress me, Manuel. I admire your patriotism. Allow me to help you in your pursuit of change, so that even the poor may finally gain the chance to study.”
Gustavo Dela Cruz, son of Governor Emilio, was destined to be wed to Señorita Felicita after college, a union meant to further strengthen the businesses of Don Fernando and Emilio. Gustavo himself was a poet—handsome, eloquent, yet somewhat arrogant.
A gathering of poets was held at the University of Santo Tomas, addressing the rights of the Filipino people. Manuel and Gustavo were both invited to represent Colegio de San Juan de Letran.
